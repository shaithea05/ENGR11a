Defining Our Needs

Project 6 - Iteration

After we initially defined our problem and came up with a few solutions, we began to delve deeper to really flush out our final solution in order to create a deliverable. This involved creating the following needs statement:

Blind dogs need a way to navigate so that they can be safe and independent.

Once we had our need statement, we moved on to defining some of the Design Principles and key features that we want our product to address. We defined them as follows:

  • Customizable Measurement — We want to be able to measure specific dogs and customize our design to fit many different dogs since nose size can vary from dog to dog even if they are the same breed. We want to include clear instructions on how to measure the dog and what to measure to create the best fit.
  • Sturdy —We want the product to be able to withstand forceful impacts, without moving around too much, and being not likely to break as the dog moves around.
  • Removable —We want this produc to be able to come off with some ease if it gets stuck on something. We do not want to choke or hurt the dog.
  • Comfort -We do not want this product to cause too much discomfort. Rather we want it to allow dogs to continue their way of life without the product acting as a burden. We expect it to have a learning curve that allows the dog to eventually use the product properly and comfortably.

    • With these things in mind, we decided that for us success would look like a halo product that does not break under a heavy impact. In order to do that, we had to order a few different materials and resources which are linked HERE. Once we completed all of this planning, we moved onto prototyping our product.

    Iterating Our Prototypes

    Project 6 - Iteration

    Prototyping is an iterative process that involves designing, building, testing, and refining prototypes in order to improve ideas and identify potential issues before finalizing a product design. Prototyping allows for experimentation, learning, and improvement, ultimately leading to the development of a successful and optimized final product. As we worked toward creating this open-source design, we began our prototyping process to finalize our Computer-aided design (CAD).

    To begin the prototyping process we took inspiration from the already existing designs: Muffin’s Halo and the Sierra Safety Hoop. Based on these designs we started our first iteration and drew a horseshoe-like shape that we would attach to a harness. This was a very rough sketch of what we envisioned for our design, but it got the idea across for us to begin moving onto actually sketching it.

    With our idea fresh in our minds, we moved on to Fusion360 to draw our CAD and print our first prototype. We had some trouble working with Fusion360, but we used our knowledge from previous assignments (the laser box design) and created the screenshot you may see below. We started this design with a circle sketch, and then extruded it. Once we had this 3-dimensional circle, we cut it in half, and added a rectangle on its edge. We then extruded the rectangle and added a hole in the middle of it to mimic the initial blueprint of our product. Then, using our previous project experience, we mirrored half of the design to the other side of the y axis to complete our design.

    Above is a screenshot from PruseSlicer of our first iteration of the Dog Halo product. Our first print was pretty straightforward. We used PLA to test out the design and did not make any adjustments to the settings. We used this first print as a baseline of our design. We wanted to use this print as something to just see where we are starting off so that we could determine our next steps based on the successes and failures of this print.

    This initial print came out pretty well. It was very sturdy, but also very bulky. The measurements were not great, but it had the general shape. After assessing our initial print, we identified areas for improvement. While the prototype was sturdy, its bulkiness created challenges in terms of the practicality of our product and its aesthetics. We realized that we needed to refine the measurements in order to ensure a better general and enhance the overall design. We also recognized the need for parameters as we wanted this design to be adaptable to different dogs. Returning to Fusion 360, we revised our model, incorporating insights from our evaluation of our first print.

    In addition to our inital design, we printed an iteration of the Sienna Hoop to get a better understanding of a structure that we know works well for a real customer's visually impaired Pomeranian. With this print we wanted to see what style would have better functionality for a dog to use. Above is a screenshot of the sliced design on PrusaSlicer with organize structures. This was the third print attempt that we made. Although not shown, the first print was done in PLA with no structure and the print ultimately failed. Our second iteration was also printed in PLA but this version has some organic structures added; this print also failed. Finally, we tried to print the hoop with PETG instead, and had a successful result. We really liked this design with the ovrehead aspect to it; however, when it came down to the testing, this showed very little promise as it was brittle but also posed a potential threat to dogs when first using it. We wanted to make sure that our product would be safe for dogs to use even if it has a learning curve, but this prodcut would have held up to that standard.

    For our next print, in our revised CAD design, we implemented parameters on key dimensions in order to introduce flexibility when adjusting the product to different dogs. We also chose to create a more elliptical shape to optimize the form for aesthetics. Moreover, we strategically reduced the thickness of the halo component to mitigate the overall bulkiness of our product, achieving a more streamlined and sleek appearance without compromising structural integrity. These adjustments reflected our commitment to continuous refinement and innovation, ensuring that our final product would meet the demands of both functionality and comfortable aesthetics

    This print was unsuccessful the first time as it had fallen off of the magnetic Prusa plate. After further inspection, we had come to the conclusion that the plate itself had too many oils on it from people handling it, and we wiped it down with some alcohol. After prepping the plate, we put it back and reprinted our design. Unfortunately, we came across the same issue where our design would not fully print. Based on this error, we increased the width of our halo so that we could reduce the impact of any collisions. Additionally, we did not have access to a dog that we could test our product on so we printed a mini dog print along with a mini version of our improved product.

    This is a screenshot of the sliced design in PrusaSlicer with a raft that we hoped would help the print successfully stick to the plate. As previously mentioned, this print was much too thin, so we increased the width which helped a little bit.

    We printed our own version of a small harness for our mini dog print and used it to attach the Halo with velcro. In our life sized design, we planned for the velcro to be tied around to minimize the chances of dogs taking them off themselves; however with the scaling of this print, we had to use the sticky aspect of the velcro to attach the Halo to our dog. We liked this design and felt that it would be sturdy enough to face different strengths of impacts, but we wanted to try printing another iteration of our design with curved parts of the ellipses in order to better take the impact.

    With this latest print we felt that by making the design have more curves, the force of any impact would be distributed better and result in less whiplash for the dog wearing the Halo. Unfortunately, this design did not print properly, but we want to continue working on improving our design to make a usable open source design.

    As shown in the sliced design, there are many gaps within this product as it prints due to its curving nature. We really wanted this design to work properly or even simply lead in a positive direction, unfortunately, it did not. With out basic knowledge of Fusion360 it was difficult to create a design that was 'squiggly' in a sense and maintain parameters that could be easily adjusted by dog owners that wanted to print and try this product for their dogs. Even with these issues, we tried to print this design, but a previously noted (and seen in the image), this print was unsuccessful. Based on our previous iteratinos along with our end goals, we decided to not move forward with this design for the rest of the project and moved on to printing the earlier design using different materials to increase the tensile strength for dogs to safely navigate themselves.

    This project allowed me to understand the real value of iterating through designs, and how beneficial it can truly be. When we first began this project we had a simple idea and printed it as it was. Until it was actually printed, we could not properly decipher the issues and make improvements. With the printed design we were able to perform tests and see what needed to be changed in order to ensure releasing the best possible product for these dogs. Without the prototyping phase my group would not have been able to see the part of our first few inital designs that were lacking. Additionally, we would not have understood how difficult the printing process for the top of the Sienna hoop would be without printing it. Prior to printing it we did want to include that to increase safety; however, once we began to actually print the product we realized how arduous it would be for users simply wanted to print it without a lot of prior knowledge. Additionally, the difficulty of managing the settings to properly print that version to fit a dog's specific measurements without failing outweighs the saefty benefits that part of the design would bring. This led us to steer away from that aspect and focus on the halo part. To summarize, there were a lot of different things that we were able to learn about how to improve our design for the Dog Halo through the prototyping phase. Whether we made one small change or completely altered the direction of our design, continuously iterating through our design had a large effect in getting us to our final design and is a step that should be used when creating any product to ensure that the final design is at its best it can be.
