Defining the Problem

Project 5 - Problem

Introducing the Problem

The problem that we are working to solve involves coming up with strategies that would relieve some of the stress faced by visually impaired animals when navigating obstacles, without risking harm to themselves. Through our research and trials, we will develop an open-source solution that will enhance their ability to navigate their surroundings safely and independently, while being cost friendly. Ultimately, our efforts will aim to improve the quality of life for visually impaired animals by providing them with effective tools and support systems.

Why?

Visually impaired dogs are not an uncommon occurance around the world. It can be caused by a variety of different factors such as genetics, old age, trauma, or medical conditions like cataracts or glaucoma. These dogs face every day as a new challenge and unfortunately there are not many solutions for them at the moment. This project delves a little deeper into the current solutions below, but this issue is very prevalent around the United States but not discussed enough. There is not a lot of research nor products to help ease the stress of these dog owners and relieve the dogs of their daily challenges. This project aims to create a solution that helps to begin a move toward helping visually impaired dogs with cost friendly solutions that beenfit both dogs and dog owners.

Existing Solutions

An existing solution that is available in the market is Muffin’s Halo. Muffin’s Halo is crafted from water-resistant vinyl tubing housing a gauge wire and featuring a cushioned wing attachment. Despite its innovative design, the main setback associated with this product is its high cost, posing a significant barrier for many pet owners seeking assistance for their visually impaired animals.

In addition to Muffin's Halo, another solution available is "Sienna’s Blindness Safety Hoop". This offers a more affordable alternative for pet owners, getting rid of some of that cost barrier present with Muffin's Halo. Unlike Muffin's Halo, Sienna’s Blindness Safety Hoop utilizes a harness attachment system rather than a cushion by the head, making it more accessible and adaptable for various pets. Additionally, the original maker employs PLA (polylactic acid) in its construction, further enhancing its affordability and sustainability compared to other materials. The largest issue we found with this design was comfort for your pet.

The Science Behind It

The existing solutions help to prevent painful bumps from running into walls, furniture and other obstacles with a decorative halo to safely alert and redirect your pooch when she runs into a hard surface. Engineered to be lightweight, comfortable, and adjustable, both solutions are designed to distribute the force of an impact in a manner that minimizes harm to the animal. Muffin’s Halo goes a step further by incorporating cushioned wings to protect the delicate neck and shoulder area of the dog, while "Sienna’s Blindness Safety Hoop" addresses the cost barrier, making it more accessible to pet owners seeking to support their visually impaired pets.

The Customer Profile

We are aiming for owners of / people who work with animals which have trouble seeing. We can find potential customers through Tufts Veterinary school. We are targeting owners of, or individuals who work with, visually impaired animals. However, due to our limitations of not being able to directly work with animals, our focus is on learning more about the animals' needs in order to design a properly functioning, cost friendly, halo design. To reach potential customers and learn more about the dogs, we plan to collaborate with institutions such as Tufts Veterinary School, leveraging their domain knowledge and thier network to identify individuals seeking assistance for animals with visual impairments.

Some Open Source Solutions

We researched some different open source hardware solution that we could use as inspiration for our project. This reserach included finding 3D Print designs such as the Sienna Hoop, along with finding a Blind Dog Bumper Adapter for Harness., and a study towards technological support for blind dog. Once we read about these solutions we determined that our product would need the following key features:

  • Detachability
  • Cushioned Back
  • Low Cost
  • Visor Leveled—allows sight, going up stairs, sniffing grass, etc
  • Allow eating
  • Recommend different materials for different dogs
  • (Maybe) Have a pole attached to the halo

    • Measuring Success

    Although we will not be able to use a pet of our own to test our product, to measure the success of our design, we will employ some testing focused on two primary factors: tensile strength and impact resilience. Success will be defined by the ability of the product to withstand bending forces without breaking, to ensure its durability during typical usage scenarios of pets hitting stnading objects while running. Additionally, we will evaluate the product's ability to withstand impact by testing the speed at which it can hit a wall before breaking and measuring the distance it bounces back afterward. This testing methodology will provide quantitative data to assess the effectiveness of the features in enhancing the product's overall performance and resilience. Testing comfort will be difficult, but we think that if we find the design to be comfortable, our pets will as well!

    Using Design Thinking Methods

    In our first meeting we talking about incorpoating the following Designs Thinking Methods:

    Empathize—We talked through the problem and made sure to include humane features to our design that would allow the four-legged users to still be able to perform daily tasks. This includes things like eating from their bowl without the removal of the device, and the ease of being detachable. We want to make sure that our four-legged friends are still able to live thier lives as they would have with the average 20/75 vision and not be held back by this new design. Whether it be the weight or feel, we also don't want the dogs to feel any sort of discomfort either.

    Prototype—We talked through creating multiple designs and printing them out with PLA on the Prusa MINI+ in order to slowly improve the actual design before printing it using our desired material(s). With these iterations we hope to learn new changes that we can make to improve our design whlie also addressing any flaws or improvements incrementally. By refining the design through successive prototypes, we hope to optimize its functionality, durability, and overall effectiveness before releasing our final product.

    We intend to learn from the Tufts Veterinary School to gain valuable insights into the logisitics of developing a new design tailored to the needs of visually impaired animals and work to produce an open source hardware solution for owners to have the opportunity to print. Furthermore, we plan to include a notes section offering tips and recommendations on adjusting the design to accommodate pets of various sizes, ensuring its adaptability and usability across different animal species.

    In order to maintain a proper structure and keep one another accountable throughout this process, as stated prior, we have written and agreed to carry out the collaboration agreement above.

    Our Solutions

    Project 5 - Problem

    We started working on our solution by printing the Sienna Safety Hoop. Printing this was difficult as there was a lot of overhang that required support structures. Our first print was about 90% successful, but there was a portion that did not print properly. In addition to that, we also felt that the print was much too small to analyze, so we decided to reprint it at a larger scale. We kept the same supports but increased the scale; however, this print was not successful. We are unsure as to whether it was due to our design or the printer itself, so we reprinted the design using PETG instead of PLA, and edited our design to reprint using PLA agian. To mitigate the issues we were met with, we added more supports and included a 2 layer raft that would take up most of the gorund surface of the print. Once we added all of this we reprinted a third time.

    This is an image of our initial print which was too small to analyze. As you can see, there is a section missing on the right side and a lot of misplaced stringing. It can also be observed that this print is much to frail to be tested on, so it did not prove to be very useful to us on our journey to designing an assistive halo for visually impaired animals.

    Potential Solutions

    Although having a halo for a dog can be a great solution, we have listed a few more solutions down below that could potentially solve this problem as well. Some of these are reasonable, some are very out there, but they all serve the same purpose of improving the quality of life to visually impaired dogs.

  • Get a guide dog for the blind dog-Just as a guide dog can support a blind human, they can work with other dogs to support them as well.
  • Lasik-Corrective eye surgery is not made just for humans, dogs can actually undergo it as well! Generally, if your dog suffers from cataracts, surgery is recommended and it has a very high success rate of 90% in dogs who recover their vision
  • Mini portable fence- Similar to a baby crawler, this would help dogs walk around without their bodies physically colliding with other objects. This baby crawler like product would allow the dog to move without the fear of hurting themselves as there would be a barrier preventing it.
  • Halo attaches with a harness-This is similar to the Muffin's Halo but this design uses a harness as well.
  • Attachable mobility cane-This design is similar to humans using a mobility cane when they have impaired vision. This would require some trianing, but once a dog is used to it this could be a great way for them to move about life.
  • Really big hat that has a long enough brim-This would also be similar to the Muffin's Halo design but it would be worn as a hat instead.

    • What Did We Learn?

    Through this process I was able to learn more about the lives of visually impaired dogs along with the many challenges thier owners face. There are too few solutions for a problem this prevalent throughout pets, especially dogs. Regardless of whether dog owners use a halo or any product of that sort, there are many adjustments they need to make to make sure that their dog is able to thrive in a safe and familiar space. There are little things like consistincy that really helps care for pets, or using their other senses. Overall, I was able to learn that our solution is only a small step within this industry and I hope to hear more about companies in this market working toward newer and more improved solutions for visually impaired dogs.
